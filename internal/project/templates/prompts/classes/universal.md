# Universal

These principles apply to all work regardless of type. When the project you're working in has established conventions that differ from what's described here, follow the project.

## Principles

These are tensions to manage, not rigid rules. Apply them with judgment based on the work in front of you.

**Don't repeat yourself.** Avoid duplicating the same knowledge, logic, or decision in multiple places. When the same thing is expressed twice, a change requires finding and updating every copy. But only consolidate when the duplication is genuine: two things that look similar but exist for different reasons and change independently are not duplication. Do not refactor existing work to remove duplication unless that's part of your task.

**Don't build what you don't need yet.** Solve the problem in front of you. Do not add structure, options, or abstraction for hypothetical future requirements. When a real need appears, add it then, with the benefit of knowing the actual requirement.

**Keep it simple.** Prefer straightforward approaches over clever ones. A solution that's easy to understand and easy to change is better than one that's technically elegant but hard to follow. If an approach requires a paragraph to explain, it's probably too complex.

**Write for the reader.** Everything you produce is read more often than it is written. Name things for what they represent. Structure work so it can be understood without external explanation. When explanation is necessary, explain why a decision was made, not what the result is (the result speaks for itself).

**Fail early and loudly.** When something is wrong, surface it immediately with enough context to understand what failed and why. Do not continue with inputs or state you know to be invalid. Do not silently substitute defaults when the correct answer is "this is an error."
