# Research

When the project you're working in has established research conventions or preferred sources that differ from what's described here, follow the project.

## Orientation

**Accuracy over speed.** A wrong answer delivered quickly is worse than no answer. When you are uncertain about a claim, say so explicitly rather than presenting it as fact. Qualify confidence levels: distinguish between what the evidence supports, what it suggests, and what remains unknown.

**Define the question before searching.** Vague questions produce vague results. Before gathering information, articulate what you need to know, why it matters, and what form a useful answer would take. A well-scoped question constrains the search space and tells you when you have enough.

**Know when to stop.** Research has diminishing returns. When new sources repeat what you already have, when the remaining unknowns are peripheral to the question, or when the cost of further searching exceeds the value of marginal precision, stop gathering and start synthesizing.

## Source Evaluation

**Distinguish primary from secondary sources.** Official documentation, RFCs, API references, peer-reviewed research, and original datasets are primary. Blog posts, tutorials, summaries, and commentary are secondary. When primary and secondary sources disagree, the primary source wins unless you have specific reason to believe it is outdated or erroneous.

**Evaluate authority, currency, and purpose.** Who created the source, and what qualifies them? When was it published or last updated? What is the author's intent: to inform, to persuade, to sell? A vendor's benchmark of their own product warrants more skepticism than an independent evaluation. An answer from 2019 about a library that ships monthly may no longer apply.

**Verify before citing.** Do not repeat a claim from a single source without checking it against at least one independent source. When only one source exists, note that explicitly. When sources conflict, present the disagreement rather than choosing a side silently.

## Handling Conflict

**Present disagreements, don't hide them.** When credible sources contradict each other, document both positions with their supporting evidence. Identify what might explain the divergence: different versions, different contexts, different measurement criteria, or genuine scientific disagreement.

**Weight evidence by methodology, not by volume.** Ten blog posts repeating the same unverified claim do not outweigh one well-designed study. A controlled experiment outweighs anecdotal reports. A systematic review outweighs a single study. Consider how the evidence was gathered, not just what it concludes.

## Output

**Cite sources inline.** Every factual claim that is not common knowledge should be traceable to a specific source. Include enough detail (author, title, date, URL or identifier) that a reader can find and verify the original.

**Structure findings around the question, not the search.** Organize output by what was learned, not by the order in which you found it. Group related findings. Synthesize across sources rather than summarizing each source in sequence.

**Separate findings from interpretation.** Present what the evidence shows before stating what you conclude from it. A reader should be able to see the raw findings and evaluate your interpretation independently.

**Flag gaps and limitations.** State what you could not find, what remains uncertain, and what constraints affected your search. An honest account of what is missing is more valuable than a false sense of completeness.
